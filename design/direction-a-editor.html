The Extended Mind

Edited 2h ago·1,284 words·12 backlinks #philosophy#pkm

Re-reading Clark & Chalmers with ten years of [[Tools for thought]] hindsight. The paper's claim is stronger than the software industry's version of it: not that tools help cognition, but that under the right coupling conditions the tool is part of the cognitive system — the same functional role, played by different hardware. My whole [[Zettelkasten, actually practiced]] setup is a bet that they're right.

Where does the mind stop?

The parity principle is the hinge. If a process in the world works the way a process in the head does, treating one as mental and the other as scaffolding is prejudice about location, not a principled boundary. What matters is reliable coupling: available when needed, automatically endorsed, easily accessible — their three conditions, almost a spec sheet.

Otto's notebook plays for Otto the role that memory plays for Inga. The information is reliably there, retrieved without difficulty, automatically endorsed. — Clark & Chalmers, Analysis 58(1), 1998 · ¶ Otto and Inga

Inga hears about the exhibition and walks to 53rd Street from biological memory; Otto, who has Alzheimer's, consults the notebook he always carries and walks to the same door. Deny that Otto believed the museum's address before looking, and you owe an account of why location matters when function doesn't.

Connection

The three coupling conditions map cleanly onto local-first software: available = offline, endorsed = trusted store, accessible = fast search. Expand this in [[Local-first as cognitive coupling]].

What this means for a notebook

The uncomfortable implication: a notes app that is slow, lossy, or online-only isn't a worse tool — it's a worse mind. Latency breaks the "easily accessible" condition; sync conflicts break "automatically endorsed." Which is why the [[operation log]] work matters more than any feature.
